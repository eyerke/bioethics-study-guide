Lesson 1.1

Introduction to Ethics & Bioethics

Why moral norms are not just rules, laws, or etiquette.

Core Definition
Ethics is the philosophical study of morality — the aspect of life concerned with evaluating actions through norms that aim to be universal, impartial, and dominant.

Three Marks of a Moral Norm

Normative Dominance
Moral norms override other norms. A law that mandates harm to a minority is illegitimate because morality outranks legality.
Universality
Moral truths apply everywhere — here, abroad, on Mars. Unlike a game's rules, there is no jurisdiction.
Impartiality
No one is morally privileged. If lying is wrong for you, it is wrong for me in the same situation.

The Four Branches of Ethics

  • Value Theory — What is good? What is worth pursuing?
  • Normative Ethics — What standards make an action right or wrong?
  • Metaethics — What makes moral claims true at all?
  • Applied Ethics — Concrete problems (this is where bioethics lives).
How to Apply This Lesson
  1. When facing a bioethics case, first check: is this a moral question, or a legal/etiquette question dressed up as one?
  2. Test your reasoning against universality and impartiality. Would you accept the conclusion if you were in the other party's position?
  3. Use logic and argument — not religion or ideology — so anyone can join the debate.
"Science can describe the facts. Only ethics can tell us what we ought to do about them."

Utilitarianism

The morality of an action lies entirely in its consequences.

The Principle of Utility
An action is morally required if, of all available actions, it maximizes the good and minimizes the bad for all those affected.

Key Points to Remember

  • Belongs to the broader family of consequentialism — intentions don't matter, only results.
  • Considers everyone affected, not just yourself. The "net" overall benefit is what counts.
  • Maximizing utility is a moral obligation, not merely permissible.
  • Classical utilitarians (Bentham, Mill) embraced hedonism: good = pleasure, bad = pain.

Worked Example — Lying

Lying on a CV
Many lose (better candidates, the company, society). One gains (you). Net negative → impermissible.
Doctor & terminal patient
If the lie spares depression and yields more joy in the patient's last months, the net result is positive → required.

Two Classic Counterexamples

The Utilitarian Surgeon. Five dying patients each need an organ. A healthy patient walks in for a checkup. Killing one to save five maximizes utility — but it is monstrous. Utility seemingly demands the unthinkable.
The Sheriff & the Mob. Framing one innocent man to satisfy a lynch mob saves many lives. Utility says yes; justice says absolutely not. The theory ignores individual rights.
How to Apply Utilitarianism
  1. List every party affected by each available action.
  2. Estimate net pleasure/pain (or well-being) for each option.
  3. Pick the action with the highest net good.
  4. Sanity-check: would the result violate basic rights or justice? If yes, the theory may be missing something.

Kantian Ethics

Morality is grounded in reason, duty, and the dignity of persons.

For Kant, only the good will — acting from the intention to do what is right because it is right — has unconditional moral worth. The morally good agent acts from duty, guided by the Categorical Imperative.

Formula I — Universal Law of Nature
Act only on those maxims that you can reasonably will become a universal law.
Formula II — Humanity
Always treat persons — yourself or others — as ends in themselves, never merely as a means.

Key Concepts

  • Maxim — the personal rule behind your action ("I will lie to get what I want").
  • Universalizable — the maxim still makes sense if everyone followed it. Lying universalized destroys trust, so it self-destructs.
  • "Merely" as a means — using someone is fine if they consent and are respected (e.g., paying a waiter). It's wrong only when their autonomy is bypassed.
  • Morality and rationality are one and the same: to be moral is to be rational.

Objections

The Sadomasochist. Universal Law fails when an agent willingly accepts being harmed in return — the maxim "I'll torture others for pleasure" can be universalized by them. Formula I can't catch every wrong.
The Lifeboat & the Inquiring Murderer. Kant forbids lying even to save a friend's life from a murderer at the door. The absoluteness of duty produces grotesque results.
How to Apply Kant
  1. State your maxim plainly: "I will do X in order to Y."
  2. Ask: could this become a universal law without contradiction?
  3. Then ask: does my action treat every person involved as a rational end, never as a tool?
  4. If both checks pass — the action is permissible (perhaps required).

Virtue Ethics

What would a virtuous person — acting in character — do?

The Core Claim
An act is morally right just because it is one a virtuous person, acting in character, would do in that situation.

Inherited from Socrates, Plato, and especially Aristotle, virtue ethics judges persons first and actions only indirectly. The goal is to become good, not merely to follow a rule.

Key Ideas

  • Virtue — an excellent, deeply embedded trait of character (honesty, courage, justice, wisdom, benevolence, self-control).
  • Moral exemplar — an idealized person whose dispositions guide our judgment.
  • The Golden Mean — virtues sit between two vices. Courage lies between cowardice and recklessness.
  • Phronesis (practical wisdom) — the master virtue that knows when and how to apply the others.

Three Cashiers — Only One is Honest

PersonWhy she doesn't stealTruly virtuous?
JoanAfraid of getting caughtNo
JaneFollowing the rulesNo
JeanThe thought sickens her — stealing is simply not who she isYes

Virtue isn't action — it's the whole architecture of feelings, reactions, and choices that produce the action.

Strength: Flexibility

Where Utilitarianism forces the surgeon to harvest organs and Kant forces you to tell the murderer the truth, the virtuous person knows: be magnanimous, but not reckless; be honest, unless honesty endangers the innocent.

Objection. Who counts as a moral exemplar? What if two virtuous agents disagree? Virtue ethics gives less concrete decision-procedure than its rivals.
How to Apply Virtue Ethics
  1. Identify the relevant virtues at stake (courage? honesty? compassion?).
  2. Ask: what would a person who fully possesses these traits — not merely fakes them — actually do here?
  3. Aim for the golden mean between extremes; rely on practical wisdom in the gray zone.

Divine Command & Natural Law

Two religiously-rooted answers — and the dilemmas they face.

Divine Command Theory (DCT)
An act is morally required just because God commands it, and immoral just because God forbids it.

The Euthyphro Dilemma

Plato's challenge: Does God command an action because it is right, or is it right because God commands it?

Horn 1
If God commands it because it's right, then morality is grounded in something other than God's commands. DCT collapses.
Horn 2
If it's right because God commands it, morality becomes arbitrary — God could make murder good by decree.
Natural Law Theory (Aquinas)
An act is morally right just because it is consistent with the Natural Law — the rational order built into our nature.

Core Commitments

  • All things have natural purposes (telos). For humans: life, procreation, knowledge, society, seeking God.
  • Acts that promote these purposes are good; those that frustrate them are wrong.
  • Reason is our God-given tool for discovering this law — the theory is accessible to anyone, religious or not.
  • Avoids the Euthyphro dilemma: God doesn't command arbitrarily — He creates a nature with morality embedded in it.

Two Famous Objections (Hume's Is–Ought Gap)

What's natural isn't always good. Sexual aggression may "naturally" propagate genes, but it is plainly evil.
What's unnatural isn't always bad. Wearing glasses, using contraception, or wearing headphones thwarts a "natural" purpose without any wrongness.
How to Apply These Theories
  1. For DCT: identify the source command and ask whether it survives the Euthyphro test.
  2. For Natural Law: identify the natural purpose at stake and ask whether the act fulfills or frustrates it.
  3. Always be ready to challenge the move from "is" to "ought" — Hume's warning.

Lesson 2.1

The Belmont Report

How public outrage built the foundations of modern bioethics.

In 1974 Congress created the National Commission for the Protection of Human Subjects. Its 1976 Belmont Report articulated three principles that govern modern research and clinical ethics.

1. Respect for Persons
Acknowledge people's capacity for self-determination and protect those with diminished autonomy.
2. Beneficence
Do no harm; maximize possible benefits and minimize possible harms.
3. Justice
Distribute benefits and burdens of research equitably, free of discrimination and bias.

The Cases that Forced the Principles

Nazi Medical Experiments (WWII). Prisoners and children subjected to lethal experiments without consent. The aftermath produced the Nuremberg Code (1947) — voluntary consent, no high-risk-of-death experiments, social value required.
Tuskegee Study (1932–1972). The U.S. Public Health Service watched 399 African American men suffer untreated syphilis for 40 years — even after penicillin existed. 28 died of syphilis directly; 100 from complications; 40 wives infected; 19 children born with congenital syphilis. The outrage produced the Belmont Report.
"Both cases failed all three principles at once: autonomy denied, harm inflicted, and burdens dumped on the disadvantaged."
How to Apply Belmont
  1. Run every research design or clinical action through all three principles.
  2. Ask: did the participants give informed, voluntary consent? Are benefits proportional to harms? Are vulnerable populations being singled out unjustly?
  3. If even one principle is violated, the act is impermissible — even if outcomes are good.

Lesson 2.2

Autonomy & Paternalism

When can we override a person's "free" choice — and when must we not?

Autonomy (auto + nomos = "self-rule")
The rational capacity of persons to determine their own lives, free from controlling influences.

Three Conditions of an Autonomous Decision

  • Rational competence — the person can evaluate their situation and future well-being.
  • Adequate information — accurate, complete, and understood.
  • Freedom from coercion — no undue influence, threat, or manipulation.

If any condition fails — even for a competent adult — the choice is not fully autonomous.

Paternalism: When Helping Hurts

Paternalism is interfering with another person's autonomy "for their own good." It is sometimes justified (children, severely cognitively impaired adults), but never for competent adults capable of autonomous choice — even if their decision will harm them.

Elizabeth Bouvia (the case that became "Lisa"). 25, quadriplegic from cerebral palsy, in constant pain. She entered a hospital asking for pain relief and refused to be fed. The hospital force-fed her. In 1986 the California Court of Appeal ruled she had a "constitutionally guaranteed right" to refuse treatment. Her intentions were declared irrelevant.

Watch Out: The Hippocratic Oath's Blind Spot

Hippocrates emphasized beneficence and nonmaleficence, but said nothing about respecting the patient's perspective. That makes it inherently paternalistic. Modern bioethics corrects this by adding autonomy as the equal partner of beneficence.

How to Apply
  1. Test the three conditions of autonomy before honoring (or overriding) a refusal.
  2. Distinguish lack of competence (genuine grounds for paternalism) from disagreement with the patient's values (no grounds).
  3. If a competent adult refuses care, respect it — even if the refusal will lead to their death.

Lesson 2.3

Informed Consent

Autonomy made operational in clinics and research labs.

Definition
Informed consent is the autonomous authorization of a medical intervention by an individual patient.

Three Conditions of Valid Consent

Competence
Capable of judgments about one's own healthcare and well-being.
Understanding
Not just told the facts — actually grasps their meaning and significance.
Voluntariness
Free from coercion, manipulation, or undue influence.

Consent is a Process, Not a Form

Don't confuse consent with the legal signature. The signed form is a snapshot; real consent is the ongoing conversation throughout treatment, including the patient's ability to pause or withdraw at any moment.

Disagreements about Personal Values

When doctor and patient share a goal but disagree about means, the doctor's expertise carries weight. When they disagree about values (e.g., "is bodily integrity worth dying for?"), they have equal authority. Doctors only know what is medically best, not what is best for the patient all-things-considered.

Mr. Johnson's toe. A diabetic refuses amputation of a gangrenous toe. If he refuses out of false belief about treatment (Scenarios 1 & 2) — persuade. If he refuses because bodily wholeness is core to his identity (Scenario 3) — respect. The doctor's expertise stops at the boundary of personal values.

When Consent Cannot Be Obtained

  • Ask: If the patient could decide, would they consent?
  • Involve legal guardians who genuinely consider the patient's well-being.
  • In emergencies, reasonable assumptions of consent are permitted.
How to Apply
  1. Verify all three conditions before any major intervention.
  2. Treat consent as continuous; check in and re-confirm.
  3. Separate "is this medically advisable?" from "does this match the patient's values?" — only the patient owns the second answer.

Truth-Telling & Conscientious Objection

Higgs on doctor-honesty; Wicclair on provider conscience.

Higgs: Lying to Patients is Almost Never Permissible

Roger Higgs defines lying broadly — even silence or carefully crafted half-truths intended to mislead count as deceit. He rejects three common defenses of medical lying:

Excuse for LyingHiggs's Response
"The information is too complex."Complexity does not erase the duty to inform. Patients can still make informed choices with imperfect understanding.
"Bad news is too distressing."Distress is not a license to deceive. Patients consistently report being glad they were told the truth.
"Telling the truth will harm them."The answer is better telling, not lying. Long-term harm from deception (eroded trust, bad decisions) outweighs short-term distress.
"Lying must be a last resort, and we should act as if we were to be called upon to defend the decision in public debate." — Higgs

Conscientious Objection: Three Positions

Conscience Absolutism
Providers have broad rights to refuse any procedure that violates conscience. No further obligations.
Incompatibilism
Refusing legal medical care is incompatible with professional duty. Personal conscience cannot override the role.
Reasonable Accommodation (Wicclair's middle ground)
Refusal is allowed only if the provider refers the patient to someone willing to perform the procedure, and the referral does not unduly burden the patient.
How to Apply
  1. For truth-telling: ask if the only goal of withholding is the patient's interest, and whether the same goal could be reached through more sensitive disclosure.
  2. For conscience: weigh provider integrity against patient access. Does the patient have realistic alternatives? Is the referral genuine?
  3. Remember: physicians hold a near-monopoly on legal access to many treatments — that creates a strong public duty.

Mill's Liberty & the BIID Challenge

When may society interfere with a person's choice?

The Harm Principle (Mill, On Liberty, 1859)
The only acceptable reason for interfering with individual liberty is to prevent harm to others.
Corollary
No one is ever justified in interfering in conduct that affects only the individual themself.

Mill rules out paternalism for competent adults: their good, their happiness, even what others think is "wise" or "right" are not sufficient grounds. Society may persuade, reason, plead — never compel.

Why Mill Wrote It

To defend basic liberties (speech, conscience, association) against the new threat of tyranny of the majority in democratic societies.

The BIID Test Case

Body Integrity Identity Disorder: a rare condition in which a person experiences a healthy limb as not their own and seeks amputation. Therapy and medication are largely ineffective. If denied, sufferers may attempt unsafe self-amputation.

Arguments at Stake

Against Voluntary AmputationFor Voluntary Amputation
Beneficence: Removing a healthy limb is harm; doctors must do no harm.Harm Minimization: Refusing risks worse self-harm. Surgery is the lesser evil.
Rational Desires: No reasonable person could expect amputation to improve well-being; therefore the choice isn't autonomous.Autonomy: The request is informed, long-standing, and harms no one else — exactly what Mill protects.

Why It Matters

BIID stress-tests Mill: if you accept his harm principle without exception, voluntary amputation must be permitted. If you refuse, you owe an account of where Mill's principle stops.

How to Apply Mill
  1. Identify who is actually harmed by the act. Only third-party harm justifies interference.
  2. Reject paternalism for competent adults — even when their choice seems unwise.
  3. Test edge cases (BIID, addiction, voluntary slavery) to find your real intuitions about Mill's limits.

Lesson 3.1

Healthcare in the United States

Why the question of distributive justice is urgent.

The Numbers (OECD & Commonwealth Fund)

  • The U.S. spends nearly twice the OECD average on healthcare — yet has the lowest life expectancy and highest suicide rates among 11 peer nations.
  • Highest rate of preventable hospitalizations and avoidable deaths among peers.
  • 24.3 million adults aged 19–64 were uninsured in early 2020.
  • 18.5 million insured adults experienced a coverage gap that year.
  • Nearly 1 in 10 American adults — about 23 million — owes significant medical debt.

Structural Failures

Employer-Tied Insurance
Workers have little choice over plans; lose coverage when they lose a job.
Coverage Denials
Treatments deemed "unnecessary" or "out of network" leave patients with full bills.
Price Opacity
Patients can't compare prices in advance, enabling severe price gouging.
Excluded Care
Most plans exclude dental and vision — treated as separate insurance markets.

Two Competing Visions

Healthcare as a right. Government must guarantee access; mandatory taxes fund universal coverage. Critics call the compulsion unacceptable.

Healthcare as a liberty. Government should secure liberties, not provide goods. Individuals choose their own care. Critics say this leaves the vulnerable behind.

The Question that Drives Paper III
  1. What is justice, and what is a right?
  2. What theory of distributive justice should govern healthcare?
  3. When supply is scarce, by what criteria do we choose who gets care?

Justice, Rights & Distribution

The conceptual toolkit before the theories.

Justice (broadly)
Individuals getting what is fair or what is their due. Justice = respecting rights.
A Right
A legitimate claim one person has against others that imposes an obligation to either provide a good or not interfere with its acquisition.

Negative vs. Positive Rights

Negative Rights (Liberty)Positive Rights (Welfare)
Impose a duty not to interfere.Impose a duty to provide a good or service.
Example: private property — others must not take it.Example: education — others must furnish it.
"Hands off.""Hand it over."

A right to healthcare would be a positive right: someone has the obligation to provide it.

Distributive Justice

The branch of justice concerned with how the benefits and burdens of social cooperation are distributed across members of society. The disagreement at the heart of Paper III is not whether some distribution is required — it's which distribution counts as fair.

How to Apply
  1. Whenever you hear "X has a right to Y," translate it: "Who has the obligation, and what kind of obligation is it?"
  2. Ask whether the right is positive or negative — that determines the scope of the duty.
  3. Then apply the theories on the next page.

Rawls vs. Nozick

Two giant theories of distributive justice.

Rawls — Justice as Fairness
Inequalities are permitted only when they (a) attach to positions open to all under fair equality of opportunity, and (b) benefit the least-advantaged members of society.

Rawls's Two Principles

  • 1st Principle. Each person has an equal claim to a fully adequate scheme of basic rights and liberties.
  • 2nd Principle (Difference Principle). Economic inequalities are just only if they make the worst-off better off than they'd otherwise be.

The Original Position

Imagine designing society from behind a veil of ignorance: you don't know your future race, gender, talents, wealth, or health. Rawls argues every rational designer would build a society that protects the worst-case position — including guaranteeing healthcare.

StructureLeastMiddleMostVerdict
A10k10k10kEqual but poor
B50k100k150kBest (Rawls)
C20k200k500kUnjust — bottom worse than B
Nozick — Justice as Entitlement
A distribution is just if it arose from a just process — not because it matches some preferred pattern.

Nozick's Three Just Acquisitions

  • Just Transfer — voluntary exchange between consenting parties.
  • Just Initial Acquisition — claiming unowned things, subject to the Lockean proviso ("enough and as good left for others").
  • Just Rectification — compensation for past rights violations.

Nozick's two libertarian rights: full self-ownership and the power to transfer property. From this, taxation for redistribution = forced labor. Taxes are only legitimate to fund the protection of rights themselves (police, courts, defense).

How to Apply
  1. For Rawls: Run the original-position test. Would a designer behind the veil accept this distribution? If the worst-off can be improved without worsening the best, you must improve them.
  2. For Nozick: Trace the history of holdings. Was each step a voluntary transfer or just acquisition? If so, any resulting distribution is just.
  3. Healthcare: Rawls supports a right to it; Nozick rejects compulsory healthcare funding outright.

Micro Allocation & Liver Transplants

When supply runs out, who gets care?

Rescher's Five Allocation Criteria (1969)

  • Likelihood of success — how well will the treatment work?
  • Life-expectancy after successful treatment.
  • Family role — how many depend on the patient?
  • Future contributions — what will they give society?
  • Past services rendered — what have they already given?

Caution: "Medical" criteria 1–2 unjustly favor the young and able-bodied. "Social" criteria 3–5 discriminate against solitary people and are nearly impossible to measure objectively.

Moss & Siegler — Liver Transplants for Alcoholics

They argue that patients with alcohol-related end-stage liver disease (ARESLD) should not compete equally with patients whose ESLD has other causes. Three arguments:

ArgumentClaim
FairnessNon-alcoholics never had a healthy liver; transplant is their first chance. ARESLD patients are getting a "second" liver.
Personal EffortAlcoholism is a disease, but treatment is available. Patients had 10–20 years to seek help and are responsible for not doing so.
Public SupportThe public funds organ programs and supports them less for alcoholics. Equal treatment may collapse public backing for transplantation altogether.

Two Strong Objections

The disease cuts both ways. The same biological/social factors that cause addiction also impair the ability to seek treatment. Holding patients responsible doubles the unfairness.
Selective punishment. We don't withhold care from extreme-sport athletes, traffic-accident victims, or people with obesity-related disease. Why single out alcoholism?
How to Apply
  1. Reject any criterion based on race, sex, religion, or eye color — these are paradigm cases of unjust discrimination.
  2. For each "neutral" criterion, ask: does it covertly discriminate against age, disability, family status, or class?
  3. Be consistent: if you blame alcoholics for their disease, you must also blame motorcyclists, smokers, and athletes — or none of them.

The Value of Life & Fair Innings

John Harris on age and life-saving allocation.

The Anti-Ageist Argument

We're tempted to favor the young because they have "more life to live." But none of us actually knows how long we have left. Each person's "rest of my life" has indefinite, equal value to them.

Anti-Ageist Argument
1. Both lives do not differ in value due to age.
2. If they don't differ in value, denying the older person is unjust.
3. ∴ It would be unjust to deny the older person the chance to live.

The Fair Innings Argument

But intuition resists in extreme cases (a 2-year-old vs. an 89-year-old). Harris introduces the idea of a fair innings — say, 70 years — the amount of life one can reasonably expect.

Fair Innings Argument
1. It is not reasonable to expect to live past one's fair innings at the expense of someone who has not yet reached theirs.
2. ∴ It is not unjust to save the younger person, provided they have not yet reached their fair innings.

Putting Them Together

  • If both patients are below the threshold (e.g., 20 vs. 50), age is irrelevant — fall back on anti-ageism.
  • If one is past the threshold (e.g., 89 vs. 2), priority to the younger is just.
  • The 20- vs. 50-year-old case: both would suffer the same injustice if denied. Harris's analogy: a 7-minute task; whether you get 3 or 4 minutes, both are unjust losses.
"It is always a misfortune to die when one wants to go on living, but it is not a tragedy to die in old age." — Harris
How to Apply
  1. If both candidates are well below the fair-innings threshold, do not use age as a tiebreaker.
  2. If one has clearly passed the threshold and the other has not, age may justifiably tip the scales.
  3. Don't fetishize the exact threshold — the moral idea (premature death is the real tragedy) survives any reasonable choice of number.

Genetic Testing & Manipulation

Two technologies, several new moral questions.

Two Powerful Technologies

Genetic Testing
Identifies the specific genes a person carries — including risk for hereditary disease.
Genetic Manipulation
Modifies genetic information through silencing, addition, or editing — including germline changes inherited by descendants.

Why Testing Raises Concerns

  • Knowing you have an untreatable condition (e.g., Huntington's) can cause years of anxiety before symptoms even begin.
  • It creates pressure on reproductive choices: should a carrier have children at all?
  • Many genetic diseases still have no cure — knowledge without remedy.

Why Manipulation Raises Concerns

  • Risks of experimentation in humans.
  • The blurry line between therapy (curing) and enhancement (upgrading).
  • The shadow of eugenics — state-sponsored programs once forcibly sterilized people deemed "unfit" in Nazi Germany, the U.S., Canada, Sweden, and elsewhere.
"Most people accept gene therapy. Most people reject 'designer babies.' The question is whether the line between them can survive scrutiny."

The Four Categories of Modification

TherapyEnhancement
Somatic (only this person)SGTSGE
Germline (descendants too)GLGTGLGE

The widespread view: only SGT is permissible. Resnik (Lesson 4.3) will challenge that.

How to Apply
  1. Distinguish testing from manipulation; they raise different moral questions.
  2. For any modification, identify which of the four cells (SGT, SGE, GLGT, GLGE) it occupies.
  3. Watch for the slide from voluntary parental choice into coercive eugenics.

Purdy on Reproductive Risk

Can it be wrong to have children?

Purdy's Argument
1. If an action puts a person at high risk of harm, there is a moral obligation to prevent it.
2. Procreating with high risk of transmitting a serious disease puts the possible child at high risk of harm.
3. ∴ There is a moral obligation not to procreate when there is a high risk of transmitting a serious disease.

Why Huntington's Disease?

  • Caused by a single defective gene.
  • 50% chance of inheritance from an affected parent.
  • If inherited, almost certain to develop the disease.
  • Symptoms (involuntary movement, paranoia, dementia) appear ages 30–50 — usually after the age of childbearing decisions.
  • Lasts ~15 years; ends in death.

Purdy's Replies to Three Common Pushbacks

PushbackPurdy's Reply
"Only 50% chance — that's not enough to forbid."50% chance of decades of suffering is not low. The fear and anxiety alone diminish the carrier's life.
"Some affected people are glad to be alive."They speak before facing the worst symptoms, and may not appreciate what's coming. Their voices can't override prevention.
"People have a right to procreate."The benefits of parenthood are available through adoption, sperm/egg donation, etc. The biological route isn't the only one.

A Related Obligation: The Duty to Get Tested

If you have a family history and want children, you have a duty to test yourself first. The "right not to know" loses force when another person could be seriously harmed.

Selective Abortion

Purdy thinks abortion is permissible, so prenatal testing followed by abortion is one option. But her argument doesn't depend on accepting abortion: those who reject it can fulfill the duty by preventing conception in the first place.

Discrimination Worry. Telling carriers not to procreate may communicate that lives like theirs aren't worth living. Reply: the right to life is denied only to potential people, not to real ones already alive.
How to Apply
  1. Don't equate "high risk" with "small chance." Severity × probability is what matters.
  2. If you can fulfill the same goals (parenting) through alternatives that don't risk harm, those alternatives become morally preferred.
  3. Information you'd rather not have can become a duty when another person's welfare is at stake.

Paper IV · GeneticsLesson 4.3
Lesson 4.3

Resnik on Therapy vs. Enhancement

Why the favorite line in genetic ethics may not hold.

The widespread view: gene therapy is permissible, gene enhancement is not. Resnik takes this view's five usual reasons and argues that none of them work.

The Five Alleged Differences

ClaimResnik's Verdict
Therapy promotes health; enhancement doesn't.Plastic surgery, dental braces, breast reduction don't promote health either — yet they're permissible. Health-promotion isn't required for moral permissibility.
Therapy fits the goals of medicine; enhancement doesn't."Health" itself is contested — some definitions count enhancements as therapeutic. The line is not crisp enough to do moral work.
Therapy preserves "humanness"; enhancement alters it.What counts as essentially human is unclear. Most ethical theories don't condemn changing the human form per se — only when it causes harm or disrespects autonomy.
Therapy respects unborn rights; enhancement doesn't.False. Proxy consent works for fetal surgeries; the same logic covers germline modification. An "unmodified germline" is not a meaningful right, and modification can expand a child's open future.
Therapy isn't eugenics; enhancement is.Distinguish state-sponsored from parental eugenics. Choosing a mate is already a form of parental gene-pool selection. Not all eugenics is wrong — only the coercive kind.

The Natural Law Objection — and Resnik's Reply

  • Naturalistic: natural selection perfected us, so don't tamper. Reply: evolution produces useless traits (appendix) and leaves room for improvement (immune system).
  • Theistic: we shouldn't "play God." Reply: it isn't obvious God forbids changes that promote welfare and justice.
How to Apply Resnik
  1. Don't reach for "therapy good, enhancement bad" as a quick rule. Test the specific case against real harms and consents.
  2. Evaluate enhancements case-by-case: who benefits, who consents, what are the risks?
  3. Distinguish parental eugenics (often permissible) from state eugenics (paradigm wrong because of coercion, not because of the genetic aim).

Master Application Checklist

A Universal Method for Bioethics Cases
  1. Identify the action, the parties, and the stakes. Who is affected, including future people?
  2. Run the principles. Respect for persons / beneficence / justice. Did any fail?
  3. Run the theories. What does utility say? Kant? Virtue? Natural Law? Where do they disagree?
  4. Test autonomy. Competence, information, freedom from coercion — all three?
  5. Test justice. Behind the veil, would you accept this distribution? Did the goods arrive through just transfer?
  6. Test the harm principle. Is interference justified by harm to others, or is it paternalism?
  7. Stress-test with counterexamples. The best moral conclusion survives the surgeon, the sheriff, the inquiring murderer, the BIID patient, and the original position.

Answers & Explanations

Compare your work — focus on the reasoning, not just the verdict.

  • 1. C — Impartiality. Universality is about place; impartiality is about persons.
  • 2. B. The case shows utility can demand the violation of basic rights — the theory's most famous weakness.
  • 3. B. "Merely" matters: using a waiter who consents and is paid is fine. Bypassing autonomy is not.
  • 4. Joan and Jane perform the right action for the wrong reasons (fear, rule-following). Only Jean has the deep dispositions — the feelings, reactions, and identity — that virtue ethics requires for genuine virtue.
  • 5. Horn 1: God commands actions because they are right → morality is grounded in something other than God's commands; DCT collapses. Horn 2: Actions are right because God commands them → morality is arbitrary; God could declare murder right by decree. Either way, DCT loses.
  • 6. Respect for Persons, Beneficence, Justice.
  • 7. B. Tuskegee — though Nuremberg was the international predecessor.
  • 8. (a) Rational competence; (b) Adequate, accurate information understood by the person; (c) Freedom from coercion or manipulation.
  • 9. False. Higgs explicitly rejects this. Complexity does not license deceit; patients can still make informed choices, and doctors must try harder to communicate.
  • 10. "The only acceptable reason for interfering with individual liberty is to prevent harm to others." It rules out paternalism for competent adults — their good, happiness, or even what others think is wise are insufficient reasons to compel them.
  • 11. B. Positive/welfare rights impose a duty to provide a good or service.
  • 12. A thought experiment in which designers don't know their future status (race, talent, health, wealth). Rawls argues they would protect the worst-off position — guaranteeing healthcare access — because any one of them might end up there.
  • 13. B. Only for the protection of libertarian rights themselves. Anything else, for Nozick, is "forced labor."
  • 14. Either: (a) the same disease that causes addiction also impairs the ability to seek treatment, so blame is unfair; or (b) we don't penalize extreme-sport injuries, traffic accidents, or obesity-related disease — singling out alcoholism is inconsistent.
  • 15. Age is legitimate when one patient has clearly passed the "fair innings" threshold and the other has not (e.g., 89 vs. 2). It is not legitimate when both candidates are below the threshold (e.g., 20 vs. 50) — in such cases the anti-ageist argument controls.
  • 16. (1) If an action puts a person at high risk of harm, there is a moral obligation to prevent it. (2) Procreating with high risk of transmitting a serious disease puts the possible child at high risk of harm. (3) ∴ There is a moral obligation not to procreate when there is a high risk of transmitting a serious disease.
  • 17. Because the goods of parenthood (love, raising a child) are accessible through alternatives that don't carry the risk: adoption, sperm donation, egg donation. The biological route isn't morally privileged.
  • 18. State-sponsored eugenics (coercive, immoral) and parental eugenics (choosing mates or donors). Resnik finds parental eugenics morally acceptable; the wrong of state eugenics lies in coercion, not in the genetic aim.
  • 19. False. Resnik argues exactly the opposite — the line is too blurry to bear the moral weight typically placed on it.
  • 20. Open response — strong answers identify a case (e.g., a carrier of a serious genetic disease deciding whether to procreate with limited resources): Mill protects the carrier's liberty; Rawls might mandate state-funded testing and counseling to protect the worst-off (the future child); Purdy condemns the procreation outright.
