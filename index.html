1.1of 1.5
FieldBioethics is a branch of applied ethics — the study of moral problems in biology and medicine.

Ethics & Bioethics.

Why moral norms are not the same thing as laws, etiquette, or the rules of a game.

Ethics is the philosophical study of morality — the part of life concerned with evaluating actions and discerning norms of conduct. Moral norms are not just any rules. They have three features that distinguish them from games, laws, and politeness.

  • Normative dominance. Moral norms override other norms. A law that mandates harm is illegitimate because morality outranks legality.
  • Universality. Moral truths apply everywhere — here, abroad, on Mars. Unlike a game's rules, there is no jurisdiction.
  • Impartiality. No one is morally privileged. If lying is wrong for you, it is wrong for me in the same situation.

The four branches of ethics

Value theory asks what is good. Normative ethics asks what makes actions right or wrong. Metaethics asks whether moral claims can be true at all. Applied ethics works on concrete problems — and that is where bioethics lives.

Definition
Bioethics is the application of moral reasoning to the practice of biology and medicine — abortion, euthanasia, consent, allocation, gene editing.
How to apply this lesson
  1. When facing a case, first ask: is this a moral question — or a legal, etiquette, or game-rules question dressed up as one?
  2. Test your reasoning against universality and impartiality. Would you accept the conclusion if you were in the other party's position?
  3. Use logic and argument, not religion or ideology, so anyone can join the debate.

1.2of 1.5
FoundersJeremy Bentham & John Stuart Mill — both adopted hedonism (pleasure good, pain bad) as their theory of value.

Utilitarianism.

The morality of an action lies entirely in the consequences it produces.

Utilitarianism is a form of consequentialism: the rightness of an act depends not on intentions but on outcomes. Utilitarianism specifies that the relevant outcome is the total well-being of everyone affected.

Principle of Utility
An action is morally required if, of all the actions available, it maximizes good and minimizes bad for all those affected.

Three consequences follow from this principle. First, you must consider everyone affected, not just yourself. Second, the calculation is over net utility — an action that hurts a few is justified if it benefits the many enough. Third, maximizing utility is a moral obligation, not a permission. Anything less is wrong.

The two classic counterexamples

Counterexample · The Surgeon
Five patients each need a different transplant; a healthy patient walks in for a checkup. Killing the one to save the five maximizes utility — but it is monstrous.
Counterexample · The Sheriff
A 19th-century sheriff knows a Black man is innocent but a mob will lynch many if he isn't framed. Framing one to save many maximizes utility, but the act is plainly unjust.

Both cases reveal the same weakness: utilitarianism can demand actions that violate individual rights and basic justice. A theory that recommends killing or framing the innocent is missing something important about morality.

How to apply utilitarianism
  1. List every party affected by each available action.
  2. Estimate the net well-being produced by each option.
  3. Pick the action with the highest net good.
  4. Sanity-check: does the result violate basic rights or justice? If yes, the theory may be missing something.

1.3of 1.5
Key ideaFor Kant, morality and rationality are the same thing. To be moral is to be rational.

Kantian Ethics.

Morality is grounded in reason, duty, and the dignity of persons.

Only the good will — the determination to act from moral reasons alone — has unconditional moral worth. A person with a good will recognizes that doing the right thing is their duty, and that duty is captured by the Categorical Imperative, which Kant gives us in two forms.

Formula I
Act only on those maxims that you can reasonably will to become a universal law.
Formula II
Always treat persons — yourself or others — as ends in themselves, never merely as a means.

A maxim is the personal rule behind your action. A maxim is permissible only if it can be universalized without contradiction. Lying universalized destroys the very trust that makes lying useful, so it self-destructs and is forbidden.

Treating someone "merely as a means" means bypassing their autonomy and rational ends. You can use a waiter to bring food — that is fine if she consents and you pay. The wrong is in the word merely: when their will is ignored or overridden.

Where Kant strains

Counterexample · The Inquiring Murderer
A murderer at the door asks where your friend is hiding. Kant says you cannot lie even to save the friend's life. The absoluteness of duty produces a grotesque result.
Counterexample · The Sadomasochist
An agent who enjoys both giving and receiving pain can universalize a maxim of mutual harm. Formula I, on its own, can't catch every wrong.
How to apply Kant
  1. State your maxim plainly: "I will do X in order to Y."
  2. Ask: could this become a universal law without contradiction?
  3. Then ask: does my action treat every person involved as a rational end, never as a tool?
  4. If both checks pass, the action is permissible — perhaps required.

InheritanceFrom Socrates, Plato, and especially Aristotle's Nicomachean Ethics — written as a guide for becoming good.

Virtue Ethics.

What would a virtuous person — acting in character — do here?

Where utilitarianism judges consequences and Kant judges maxims, virtue ethics judges persons. Actions are right only indirectly — because they are what a person of true virtue would do.

Core Claim
An act is morally right just because it is one a virtuous person, acting in character, would do in that situation.

A virtue is an excellent, deeply embedded trait of character — honesty, courage, justice, wisdom, benevolence, self-control. A truly virtuous person doesn't just act honestly; she has the feelings, reactions, and dispositions of an honest person all the way down.

Three cashiers, one virtue

Joan
Doesn't steal from the register because she's afraid of being caught.
Jane
Doesn't steal because the rules say not to.
Jean
Has never even considered stealing. The thought sickens her — stealing is simply not who she is. Only Jean has the virtue.

Aristotle adds that virtue is the golden mean between two vices. Courage sits between cowardice and recklessness. Knowing the mean in any situation requires phronesis — practical wisdom — the master virtue that tells you when and how to apply the others.

Strength: flexibility

The utilitarian surgeon and the inquiring murderer trap their respective theories. Virtue ethics doesn't get caught: be magnanimous, but not reckless; be honest, unless honesty endangers the innocent. The virtuous person knows the difference.

How to apply virtue ethics
  1. Identify the virtues at stake — honesty, courage, compassion, justice.
  2. Ask: what would a person who fully embodies these traits — not merely fakes them — do here?
  3. Aim for the golden mean. Rely on practical wisdom in the gray zone.

Hume's gapJust because something is the case does not mean it ought to be. The basic challenge to Natural Law.

Divine Command
& Natural Law.

Two religiously rooted answers — and the dilemmas they face.

Divine Command
An act is morally required just because God commands it, and immoral just because God forbids it.

The Euthyphro dilemma

Plato's challenge: Does God command an action because it is right, or is it right because God commands it? Either answer breaks the theory.

First horn
If God commands what is already right, then morality is grounded in something other than God's commands. DCT collapses.
Second horn
If actions are right because God commands them, morality is arbitrary — God could declare murder good by decree.
Natural Law
An act is morally right just because it is consistent with the rational order built into our nature.

Aquinas grounds morality in nature: things have natural purposes. For humans these include life, procreation, knowledge, society, and seeking God. Acts that fulfill these are good; acts that frustrate them are wrong. Reason is the tool we use to discover what the natural law requires.

Natural law avoids the Euthyphro dilemma: God doesn't command arbitrarily, but creates a nature with morality embedded in it. Yet two famous objections (David Hume, 18th c.) press hard against it.

  • What's natural isn't always good. Sexual aggression may "naturally" propagate genes — but is plainly evil.
  • What's unnatural isn't always bad. Wearing glasses, contraception, or headphones thwarts a natural purpose without any wrongness.
How to apply
  1. For DCT: name the source command and see if it survives the Euthyphro test.
  2. For Natural Law: identify the natural purpose at stake; ask whether the act fulfills or frustrates it.
  3. Always be ready to challenge the move from "is" to "ought" — Hume's warning.

Part II — Principles of PracticeLesson 2.1
2.1of 2.5
YearThe Belmont Report was published in 1976 — two years after the U.S. Congress created the National Commission for the Protection of Human Subjects.

The Belmont
Report.

Three principles built from public outrage at unethical research.

The Belmont Report was not written in the abstract. It was written in response to documented atrocities — and it codifies three principles that now govern modern research and clinical ethics.

  • Respect for persons. Acknowledge the capacity for self-determination; protect those whose autonomy is diminished.
  • Beneficence. Do no harm; maximize possible benefits and minimize possible harms.
  • Justice. Distribute benefits and burdens of research equitably, free from discrimination and bias.

The cases that forced the principles

Nazi Medical Experiments (WWII)
Prisoners and children subjected to lethal experiments without consent. The aftermath produced the Nuremberg Code (1947): voluntary consent, no high-risk-of-death experiments, social value required.
Tuskegee Study (1932 — 1972)
The U.S. Public Health Service watched 399 African American men suffer untreated syphilis for 40 years — even after penicillin existed. 28 died of syphilis directly; 100 of complications; 40 wives infected; 19 children born with congenital syphilis. The outrage produced Belmont.

Both failed all three principles at once: autonomy denied, harm inflicted, and burdens dumped on the disadvantaged.

How to apply Belmont
  1. Run every research design or clinical action through all three principles.
  2. Ask: was consent informed and voluntary? Are benefits proportional to harms? Are vulnerable groups singled out unjustly?
  3. If even one principle is violated, the act is impermissible — even if the outcomes look good.

Part II — Principles of PracticeLesson 2.2
2.2of 2.5
EtymologyAutonomy = autos (self) + nomos (rule, law). Self-rule.

Autonomy &
Paternalism.

When may we override a person's choice — and when must we not?

Autonomy
The rational capacity of persons to determine their own lives, free from controlling influences.

Even competent adults don't make autonomous decisions all the time. For a decision to count as autonomous, it must meet three conditions.

  • Rational competence. The person can evaluate their situation and future well-being.
  • Adequate information. Accurate, complete, and actually understood.
  • Freedom from coercion. No undue influence, threat, or manipulation.

If any single condition fails, the choice is not autonomous — even when the chooser is a competent adult.

Paternalism, justified and not

Paternalism is interfering with someone's autonomy "for their own good." It can be justified for children or for adults whose cognitive capacities are seriously diminished. It is not justified for competent adults — even if their refusal will lead to their death.

Elizabeth Bouvia (1986)
A 25-year-old quadriplegic in chronic pain entered a public hospital asking for pain relief but refusing to be fed. The hospital force-fed her. The California Court of Appeal ruled she had a "constitutionally guaranteed right" to refuse treatment. Her intentions were declared irrelevant.

Note the historical blind spot. The Hippocratic Oath emphasizes beneficence and non-maleficence but says nothing about respecting the patient's perspective. That makes it inherently paternalistic — and modern bioethics adds autonomy as the equal partner of beneficence.

How to apply
  1. Test the three conditions before honoring or overriding any refusal.
  2. Distinguish lack of competence (real grounds for paternalism) from disagreement with values (no grounds at all).
  3. If a competent adult refuses care, respect it — even when the refusal will lead to death.

2.3of 2.5
Watch outDon't confuse the signed consent form with consent itself. The form is a snapshot. Real consent is ongoing.

Informed
Consent.

Autonomy made operational in clinics and research labs.

Definition
Informed consent is the autonomous authorization of a medical intervention by an individual patient.

The three conditions of consent mirror the three conditions of autonomous decisions.

  • Competence. The patient is capable of judgments about their own healthcare and well-being.
  • Understanding. Not just told the facts — actually grasps their meaning.
  • Voluntariness. Free from coercion, manipulation, or undue influence.

Consent is a process, not a form. The signed paper is a legal snapshot; real consent is the ongoing conversation throughout treatment, including the patient's ability to pause or withdraw at any moment.

Means versus values

When doctor and patient disagree, the disagreement matters. If they share a goal but differ on the means (which treatment is effective), the doctor's expertise carries weight. But if they differ on values — what counts as a life worth living — they have equal authority. The doctor knows what is medically best, but only the patient knows what is best for the patient, all things considered.

Mr. Johnson's toe
A diabetic refuses amputation of a gangrenous toe. If he refuses out of false belief about treatment, persuade. If he refuses because bodily wholeness is core to his identity, respect. Expertise stops at the boundary of personal values.

When consent cannot be obtained

Ask: If the patient could decide, would they consent? Involve guardians who genuinely consider the patient's well-being. In emergencies, reasonable assumptions of consent are permitted.

How to apply
  1. Verify all three conditions before any major intervention.
  2. Treat consent as continuous; check in and re-confirm.
  3. Separate "is this medically advisable?" from "does this match the patient's values?" — only the patient owns the second answer.

AuthorsRoger Higgs on truth-telling. Mark Wicclair on conscientious objection.

Truth &
Conscience.

Higgs on lying to patients. Wicclair on the limits of refusing care.

Roger Higgs defines lying broadly — even silence intended to mislead counts as deceit. He rejects the three most common defenses of medical lying.

  • "It's too complex to explain." Complexity doesn't erase the duty to inform. Patients can still make informed choices with imperfect understanding.
  • "Bad news is too distressing." Distress isn't a license for deception. Patients consistently report being glad they were told.
  • "The truth will harm them." The answer is better telling, not lying. Long-term harm from broken trust outweighs short-term distress.
"Lying must be a last resort, and we should act as if we were to be called upon to defend the decision in public debate." — Higgs

Conscientious objection — three positions

Mark Wicclair distinguishes three views on whether providers may refuse to perform legal procedures that violate their values.

Conscience absolutism
Providers have broad rights to refuse. No further obligations.
Incompatibilism
Refusing legal care is incompatible with professional duty. Personal conscience cannot override the role.
Reasonable accommodation (Wicclair's middle ground)
Refusal is allowed only if the provider refers the patient to someone willing to perform the procedure, and the referral does not unduly burden the patient.
How to apply
  1. For truth-telling: ask whether the only goal of withholding is the patient's interest, and whether the same goal could be achieved through more sensitive disclosure.
  2. For conscience: weigh provider integrity against patient access. Does the patient have realistic alternatives? Is the referral genuine?
  3. Remember: physicians hold a near-monopoly on legal access to many treatments — that creates a strong public duty.

SourceJohn Stuart Mill, On Liberty (1859) — written to defend basic freedoms against the "tyranny of the majority" in democratic societies.

Mill's Liberty
& the BIID Test.

When may society interfere with a person's choice?

Harm Principle
The only acceptable reason for interfering with individual liberty is to prevent harm to others.
Corollary
No one is ever justified in interfering in conduct that affects only the individual themself.

Mill rules out paternalism for competent adults: their good, their happiness, even what others judge wise — none are sufficient grounds. Society may persuade, reason, plead. It may not compel.

The BIID test case

Body Integrity Identity Disorder is a rare condition in which a person experiences a healthy limb as not their own, and seeks medical amputation. Therapy is largely ineffective. Without help, sufferers often attempt unsafe self-amputation.

BIID is the stress test for Mill. If you accept the harm principle without exception, voluntary amputation must be permitted. If you don't, you owe an account of where Mill stops.

Four arguments at stake

Against
Beneficence: Removing a healthy limb is harm; doctors must do no harm. Rational desires: No reasonable person could expect amputation to improve well-being, so the choice isn't autonomous.
For
Harm minimization: Refusing risks worse self-harm. Surgery is the lesser evil. Autonomy: The request is informed, long-standing, and harms no one else — exactly what Mill protects.
How to apply Mill
  1. Identify who is actually harmed. Only third-party harm justifies interference.
  2. Reject paternalism for competent adults — even when their choice seems unwise.
  3. Test edge cases (BIID, addiction, voluntary risk) to find your real intuitions about Mill's limits.

Part III — Justice in HealthcareLesson 3.1
3.1of 3.5
SourcesOECD comparative data and Commonwealth Fund reports on U.S. coverage and outcomes.

Healthcare in
the United States.

Why the question of distributive justice is urgent.

The U.S. spends nearly twice as much on healthcare as the average OECD country — and gets the worst outcomes among its peers. The numbers frame everything else in this part.

  • The U.S. has the lowest life expectancy and highest suicide rates among 11 peer nations.
  • Highest rate of preventable hospitalizations and avoidable deaths in the comparison group.
  • 24.3 million adults aged 19–64 were uninsured in early 2020.
  • 18.5 million insured adults experienced a coverage gap that year.
  • Nearly 1 in 10 American adults — about 23 million — owes significant medical debt.

Why insurance fails even the insured

U.S. insurance is tied to employers. Workers lose coverage when they lose a job. Plans deny services as "unnecessary" or "out of network." Patients can't compare prices in advance, which enables severe price gouging. Most plans exclude dental and vision entirely.

Two competing visions

Healthcare as a right
Government must guarantee access; mandatory taxes fund universal coverage. Critics call the compulsion unacceptable.
Healthcare as a liberty
Government should secure liberties, not provide goods. Individuals choose their own care. Critics say this leaves the vulnerable behind.
The questions that drive this part
  1. What is justice, and what is a right?
  2. What theory of distributive justice should govern healthcare?
  3. When supply is scarce, by what criteria do we choose who gets care?

3.2of 3.5
TranslationWhenever you hear "X has a right to Y" — translate it: "Who has the obligation, and what kind?"

Justice, Rights
& Distribution.

The conceptual toolkit before the theories.

Justice
Individuals getting what is fair or what is their due. Justice is about respecting rights.
A Right
A legitimate claim one person has against others that imposes an obligation either to provide a good or not to interfere with its acquisition.

Negative versus positive rights

Negative (liberty) right
Imposes a duty not to interfere. Example: private property — others must not take it. "Hands off."
Positive (welfare) right
Imposes a duty to provide a good or service. Example: education — others must furnish it. "Hand it over."

A right to healthcare, if it exists, is a positive right. Someone — the state, the taxpayer, the profession — has the obligation to provide it. That's why distributive justice is the relevant battleground.

Distributive justice

The branch of justice concerned with how the benefits and burdens of social cooperation are distributed across members of society. The disagreement isn't whether some distribution is required — it's which distribution counts as fair. The next two pages give us two competing answers: Rawls and Nozick.

How to apply
  1. For any "right" claim, identify the party with the obligation. No obligation, no right.
  2. Determine whether the right is positive or negative — that fixes the scope of the duty.
  3. Then choose your theory of distributive justice. The next page begins.

3.3of 3.5
BooksJohn Rawls, A Theory of Justice (1971). Robert Nozick, Anarchy, State, and Utopia (1974).

Rawls vs.
Nozick.

Two giant theories of distributive justice in direct conflict.

Rawls — Justice as Fairness
Inequalities are permitted only when they (a) attach to positions open to all under fair equality of opportunity, and (b) benefit the least-advantaged members of society.

Rawls's original position: imagine designing society from behind a veil of ignorance, not knowing your future race, gender, talents, wealth, or health. Rational designers would protect the worst-off position — including by guaranteeing healthcare — because any one of them might end up there. This is the difference principle.

The income test

Structure A
10k / 10k / 10k — equal but everyone is poor.
Structure B
50k / 100k / 150k — best for Rawls. The least-advantaged are best off.
Structure C
20k / 200k / 500k — unjust. The bottom is worse off than under B; the rich got richer at the expense of the poor.
Nozick — Justice as Entitlement
A distribution is just if it arose from a just process — not because it matches a preferred pattern.

Nozick starts from two libertarian rights: full self-ownership and the power to transfer property. From these he derives three legitimate ways to acquire holdings: just transfer (voluntary exchange), just initial acquisition (claiming the unowned, subject to Locke's proviso), and just rectification (compensation for past violations). Anything else is theft.

For Nozick, taxation for redistribution is forced labor. Taxes are only legitimate to fund the protection of rights themselves — police, courts, defense. There is no right to healthcare.

How to apply
  1. Rawls: run the original-position test. Can the worst-off be improved without making the best-off worse off? If so, you must improve them.
  2. Nozick: trace the history of holdings. Was each step just? If yes, any resulting distribution is just.
  3. Healthcare: Rawls supports a right to it; Nozick rejects compulsory funding outright.

3.4of 3.5
AuthorsNicholas Rescher proposed the five criteria in 1969. Alvin Moss and Mark Siegler argued the alcoholism case.

Allocation
& Liver Transplants.

When supply runs out, who gets care?

Rescher's five criteria

  • Likelihood of success. How well will the treatment work?
  • Life-expectancy after successful treatment.
  • Family role. How many depend on the patient?
  • Future contributions to society.
  • Past services rendered.

Even these "neutral" criteria face problems. The medical criteria (1–2) covertly favor the young and the able-bodied. The social criteria (3–5) discriminate against solitary people and are nearly impossible to measure objectively. Each must be defended on its own.

Moss & Siegler — alcoholism & livers

They argue that patients with alcohol-related end-stage liver disease (ARESLD) should not compete equally with patients whose ESLD has other causes. Three arguments:

  • Fairness. Non-alcoholics never had a healthy liver; transplant is their first chance. ARESLD patients are getting a "second" liver.
  • Personal effort. Alcoholism is a disease, but treatment is available. Patients had 10–20 years to seek help and are responsible for not doing so.
  • Public support. The public funds organ programs and supports them less for alcoholics. Equal treatment may collapse public backing for transplantation altogether.

Two strong objections

Objection · The disease cuts both ways
The same biological and social factors that cause addiction also impair the ability to seek treatment. Holding patients responsible doubles the unfairness.
Objection · Selective punishment
We don't withhold care from extreme-sport athletes, traffic-accident victims, smokers, or people with obesity-related disease. Why single out alcoholism?
How to apply
  1. Reject any criterion based on race, sex, religion, or eye color — paradigm cases of unjust discrimination.
  2. For each "neutral" criterion, ask: does it covertly discriminate against age, disability, family status, or class?
  3. Be consistent. If you blame alcoholics for their disease, you must also blame motorcyclists, smokers, and athletes — or none of them.

Part III — Justice in HealthcareLesson 3.5
3.5of 3.5
AuthorJohn Harris, "The Value of Life" — locates a non-arbitrary reason for favoring the young.

The Value of Life
& Fair Innings.

When is age a legitimate factor in resource allocation?

The anti-ageist argument

We're tempted to favor the young because they have "more life to live." But none of us actually knows how long we have left. Each person's "rest of my life" has indefinite, equal value to them.

Anti-ageist argument
  1. Both lives do not differ in value due to age.
  2. If they don't differ in value, denying the older person is unjust.
  3. It would be unjust to deny the older person the chance to live.

The fair innings argument

But intuition resists in extreme cases — a 2-year-old vs. an 89-year-old. Harris introduces the idea of a fair innings: the amount of life one can reasonably expect. Say, 70 years. To die before completing it is a tragedy; to die past it is a misfortune but not unjust.

Fair innings argument
  1. It is not reasonable to expect to live past one's fair innings at the expense of those who have not reached theirs.
  2. It is not unjust to save the younger person, provided they have not yet reached their fair innings.

How they fit together

If both candidates are below the threshold (e.g., 20 vs. 50), age is irrelevant — fall back on anti-ageism. If one is past the threshold and the other is not (89 vs. 2), priority to the younger is just. The 20-vs-50 case looks tempting but Harris resists: both would suffer the same injustice if denied, since neither has reached the threshold.

"It is always a misfortune to die when one wants to go on living, but it is not a tragedy to die in old age." — Harris
How to apply
  1. If both candidates are well below the threshold, do not use age as a tiebreaker.
  2. If one has clearly passed the threshold and the other has not, age may justifiably tip the scales.
  3. Don't fetishize the exact number — the moral idea (premature death is the real tragedy) survives any reasonable choice.

Part IV — Genetics & Future PersonsLesson 4.1
4.1of 4.3
ScaleThe human genome contains nearly 3 billion DNA letters encoding instructions for every cell in the body.

Genetic Testing
& Manipulation.

Two technologies, several new moral questions.

Why testing raises concerns

The ability to know one's genes can bring foreknowledge of incurable disease. Consider Huntington's disease: symptoms strike between 30 and 50 and are irreversible. Many people would prefer not to know — and yet, if they want children, the question of testing becomes morally urgent.

Why manipulation raises more

Manipulation lets us silence, add, or edit genes — including in germ cells, which means changes pass to all descendants. The promise: cure hereditary disease at the source. The fear: a return to eugenics, the practice of "improving" the gene pool that, in the 19th and 20th centuries, took the form of forced sterilization in Nazi Germany, the U.S., Canada, and Sweden.

The four-cell map of modification

Therapy
SGT — Somatic genetic therapy. Affects only the patient.

GLGT — Germline genetic therapy. Affects all descendants too.
Enhancement
SGE — Somatic genetic enhancement. Goes beyond cure.

GLGE — Germline genetic enhancement. Inherited and beyond cure.

The widespread view: only SGT is permissible. Resnik will challenge that on the next page but one.

"Most people accept gene therapy. Most people reject 'designer babies.' The question is whether the line between them can survive scrutiny."
How to apply
  1. Distinguish testing from manipulation. They raise different moral questions.
  2. For any modification, identify which of the four cells (SGT, SGE, GLGT, GLGE) it occupies.
  3. Watch for the slide from voluntary parental choice into coercive eugenics.

Purdy on
Reproductive Risk.

Can it ever be wrong to have biological children?

Purdy's argument
  1. If an action puts a person at high risk of harm, there is a moral obligation to prevent it.
  2. Procreating with high risk of transmitting a serious disease puts the possible child at high risk of harm.
  3. There is a moral obligation not to procreate when there is a high risk of transmitting a serious disease.

Why Huntington's disease

Caused by a single defective gene. A child of a carrier has a 50% chance of inheriting it. If inherited, the disease is virtually certain. Symptoms (involuntary movement, paranoia, dementia) appear at 30 to 50 — usually after the age of childbearing decisions. The illness lasts about 15 years. It ends in death.

Three replies to common pushbacks

"Only 50% — not enough"
A 50% chance of decades of suffering is not low. The fear and anxiety alone diminish the carrier's life.
"Some are glad to be alive"
They speak before facing the worst symptoms. Their voices can't override prevention.
"People have a right to procreate"
The benefits of parenthood are accessible through alternatives that don't carry the risk: adoption, sperm donation, egg donation. The biological route isn't morally privileged.

The right not to know

If you have a family history and want children, you have a duty to be tested. The "right not to know" loses its force when another person could be seriously harmed by your ignorance.

How to apply
  1. Don't equate "high risk" with "small chance." Severity multiplied by probability is what matters.
  2. If alternatives can fulfill the same goals (parenting) without the risk, those alternatives become morally preferred.
  3. Information you'd rather not have can become a duty when another person's welfare is at stake.

Resnik on Therapy
vs. Enhancement.

Why the favorite line in genetic ethics may not hold.

The standard view: gene therapy is permissible, gene enhancement is not. Resnik takes the five usual reasons offered for that line and argues none of them work.

The five alleged differences

  • Therapy promotes health; enhancement doesn't. But plastic surgery, dental braces, and male breast reduction don't promote health either — and we permit them.
  • Therapy fits the goals of medicine; enhancement doesn't. "Health" itself is contested; different definitions classify the same intervention differently.
  • Therapy preserves humanness; enhancement alters it. What counts as essentially human is unclear, and most ethical theories don't condemn changing the human form per se.
  • Enhancement violates the rights of the unborn. Resnik calls this false. Proxy consent already covers fetal surgery; the same logic covers germline modification, which can also expand a child's open future, not restrict it.
  • Enhancement is eugenics. Distinguish state-sponsored eugenics (coercive, immoral) from parental eugenics (mate choice, sperm/egg donation). The latter is routine and morally unobjectionable.

The natural law objection

Could natural law ground the prohibition? Resnik considers two readings.

  • Naturalistic version: Natural selection has perfected our nature; don't tamper. Reply: evolution leaves room for improvement (the immune system) and produces useless traits (the appendix).
  • Theistic version: We shouldn't "play God." Reply: it isn't obvious God forbids changes that promote welfare and justice.
"The line between therapy and enhancement is real but morally blurry — too blurry to bear the full weight of permissibility."
How to apply Resnik
  1. Don't reach for "therapy good, enhancement bad" as a quick rule. Test each case against real harms and consents.
  2. Evaluate enhancements case-by-case: who benefits, who consents, what are the risks?
  3. Distinguish parental eugenics (often permissible) from state eugenics (paradigm wrong because of coercion).

Building a
Strong Argument.

How to write the midterm essay — and any philosophy paper.

A philosophy essay is not a summary. It is a defended position. The grader is looking for one thing above all: did you make a clear argument that survives the strongest objection you can imagine?

An argument
A set of premises offered in support of a conclusion. If the premises are true and the logic is good, the conclusion must follow.

The five-move structure

  • Stake your claim early. One sentence in the introduction that names exactly what you will defend. No suspense. No "in this paper I will explore."
  • Set up the terms. Define the technical concepts you'll use (autonomy, harm principle, fair innings) before you use them. Cite the philosopher who introduced them — in your own words.
  • Build your main argument. State your premises explicitly. Show the logical connection. Use a real example to make the abstract concrete.
  • Confront the strongest objection. Not the easiest one. Find the move a smart opponent would make and answer it on its own terms.
  • Conclude with what survived. End not with a summary but with the modified version of your claim that endured the objection.

What strong philosophical writing avoids

  • The list trap. Five shallow points lose to one deep one. Pick your battle.
  • Quotations. Show mastery by paraphrasing in your own words. Quote only when the wording itself is the point.
  • Appeals to feeling. "I think" is fine; "this just seems wrong" is not. Replace intuitions with reasons.
  • Strawmen. If your opponent's view sounds stupid in your essay, you've misrepresented it. Steelman first, refute second.
  • Vagueness. Concrete cases beat abstract gestures every time.
A reusable essay template
  1. Para 1. State the question, the answer you defend, and a one-sentence preview of the reason.
  2. Para 2. Define your terms and frame the concept (Mill's harm principle, Rawls's veil, etc.).
  3. Paras 3–4. Lay out the argument with explicit premises and one concrete example.
  4. Para 5. Raise the strongest objection a smart reader would make. Name it.
  5. Para 6. Reply on its own terms. Concede what you must; defend what survives.
  6. Para 7. Restate your modified claim. End on the strongest line you can write.

Argument Moves
That Score.

A toolkit of philosophical maneuvers you can deploy in any essay.

Standard form

When defending or attacking a thesis, lay out the argument in premise–conclusion form. It forces clarity. The Purdy and Harris arguments on the previous pages are examples — short, numbered, with the conclusion clearly marked.

Template
  1. Premise one — typically a moral principle.
  2. Premise two — a factual claim that triggers the principle.
  3. The conclusion that follows when both are accepted.

The counterexample move

The single most powerful tool in ethics. Find a case where your opponent's principle yields an obviously wrong verdict. The utilitarian surgeon, the inquiring murderer, BIID, the framed innocent — each is a counterexample weaponized against a theory.

To wield it well: describe the case vividly, show what the theory predicts, then point out the predicted verdict is intolerable. Don't just gesture — make the reader feel the wrongness.

The reductio ad absurdum

Take your opponent's premise seriously and follow it all the way down. If it leads somewhere absurd, the premise must be false. This is exactly what the surgeon case does to utility: if we maximize utility, then we kill the healthy patient — and that's monstrous, so utility-maximization can't be the whole story.

The "even granting" move

"Even granting that X is true, the conclusion still doesn't follow." This concedes the opponent's strongest premise and shows the argument still fails. It's harder than refusing the premise, but it's far more convincing.

The distinction move

When two cases look alike but pull in different directions, find a morally relevant difference. Resnik does this when he splits eugenics into state-sponsored (coercive, wrong) and parental (voluntary, fine). The whole essay turns on the distinction.

The analogy move

"This case is morally just like that one — and we already agree about that one." Used well, it transfers an intuition from a settled case to a contested one. Used badly, it forces a fit that isn't there.

Three rules for the night before the exam
  1. Memorize one strong counterexample for each major theory. They are your single most useful exam tools.
  2. Write your thesis statement before you write anything else. If it's vague, your essay will be vague.
  3. Leave 5 minutes at the end to write a sharp final sentence. Endings are remembered.

Answer Key.

With brief explanations for each question.

  • 1. C — Impartiality. Universality is about place; impartiality is about persons.
  • 2. Utility can demand the violation of basic rights — the theory's most famous weakness.
  • 3. "Merely" means bypassing the person's autonomy and rational ends. Using a waiter who consents and is paid is fine; the wrong is in disregarding will.
  • 4. Joan and Jane do the right action for the wrong reasons (fear, rule-following). Only Jean has the deep dispositions — the feelings and identity — that virtue ethics requires.
  • 5. Horn 1: God commands actions because they're right → morality is grounded elsewhere; DCT collapses. Horn 2: Actions are right because God commands → morality is arbitrary. Either way, DCT loses.
  • 6. Respect for Persons, Beneficence, Justice.
  • 7. B — Tuskegee. Nuremberg was the international predecessor; Tuskegee directly triggered Belmont in the U.S.
  • 8. (a) Rational competence; (b) Adequate, accurate information understood by the person; (c) Freedom from coercion or manipulation.
  • 9. False. Higgs explicitly rejects this. Complexity does not license deceit; doctors must try harder to communicate.
  • 10. "The only acceptable reason for interfering with individual liberty is to prevent harm to others." It rules out paternalism for competent adults — their good or happiness are insufficient grounds to compel them.
  • 11. B. Positive/welfare rights impose a duty to provide a good or service.
  • 12. A thought experiment in which designers don't know their future status. Rawls argues they would protect the worst-off — guaranteeing healthcare access — because any of them might end up there.
  • 13. Only to fund institutions that protect libertarian rights (police, courts, military). Anything else, for Nozick, is "forced labor."
  • 14. Either: (a) the same factors that cause addiction also impair the ability to seek treatment; or (b) we don't penalize extreme-sport injuries, smoking, or obesity-related disease — singling out alcoholism is inconsistent.
  • 15. Legitimate when one patient has clearly passed the fair-innings threshold and the other has not (e.g., 89 vs. 2). Not legitimate when both candidates are below the threshold (20 vs. 50) — the anti-ageist argument controls.
  • 16. (1) High risk of harm → moral obligation to prevent. (2) Procreating with high risk of serious disease puts the possible child at high risk of harm. (3) Therefore, obligation not to procreate.
  • 17. The goods of parenthood are accessible through alternatives that don't carry the risk: adoption, sperm donation, egg donation. The biological route isn't morally privileged.
  • 18. State-sponsored (coercive, immoral) and parental (mate selection). Parental eugenics is acceptable; the wrong of state eugenics lies in coercion.
  • 19. False. Resnik argues the opposite — the line is too blurry to bear the moral weight typically placed on it.
  • 20. Open response. A strong answer picks a real case (e.g., a Huntington's carrier deciding whether to procreate with limited resources), runs each theory's verdict, picks one, defends it with reasons, and anticipates the strongest objection.
