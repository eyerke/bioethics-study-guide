Terms · Principles · Arguments
For Sophia · Midterm

Part I · Moral Theories 5

Utilitarianism Bentham · Mill
Principle of Utility: an action is morally required if it maximizes good and minimizes bad for all those affected. Classical form adopts hedonism (pleasure good, pain bad). Weakness: Surgeon, Sheriff cases — utility can demand injustice.
Kantian Ethics Immanuel Kant
Only the good will has moral worth. The Categorical Imperative: F-I (Universal Law) act only on maxims you can will to be universal. F-II (Humanity) treat persons as ends, never merely as means. Weakness: Inquiring Murderer, Lifeboat.
Virtue Ethics Aristotle
An act is right just because a virtuous person, acting in character, would do it. Virtues = embedded character traits (honesty, courage, justice). Golden mean: virtue lies between two vices. Phronesis = practical wisdom, the master virtue.
Divine Command Theory
An act is right just because God commands it. Two problems: (1) God may not exist; (2) the Euthyphro Dilemma — either God commands what is independently right (DCT collapses) or morality is arbitrary (unacceptable).
Natural Law Theory Aquinas
An act is right if consistent with natural purpose. Human purposes: life, procreation, knowledge, society, seeking God. Reason discovers the law. Weakness: Hume's Is–Ought gap — what is natural isn't always good; what's unnatural isn't always bad.

Part II · Belmont Principles 3

Respect for Persons
Acknowledge people's capacity for self-determination; protect those whose autonomy is diminished.
Beneficence
Do no harm; maximize benefits and minimize harms.
Justice
Distribute the benefits and burdens of research equitably, free of bias.

Part II · Autonomy & Consent

Autonomy
Rational self-rule. Three conditions: competence, adequate information (understood), freedom from coercion. If any fails, the decision is not autonomous — even for competent adults.
Informed Consent
Autonomous authorization of a medical intervention. Same three conditions. Consent is a process, not a signed form.
Paternalism
Overriding autonomy "for the person's own good." Justified for children & severely impaired. Not justified for competent adults.
Mill's Harm Principle On Liberty, 1859
The only acceptable reason for interfering with individual liberty is to prevent harm to others. Their own good is not sufficient. Rules out paternalism.

Part II · Higgs & Wicclair

Higgs on Truth-Telling
Lying to patients is almost never permissible. Rejects 3 excuses: complexity, distress, protection from harm. Answer is better telling, not lying. Lying = last resort.
Wicclair's 3 Positions conscience
Absolutism: providers may always refuse. Incompatibilism: refusal incompatible with professional duty. Reasonable accommodation (middle ground): refuse only if you refer, and referral doesn't burden the patient.

Part III · Justice Rights

Negative Right
Imposes a duty not to interfere (e.g., private property). "Hands off."
Positive Right
Imposes a duty to provide a good or service (e.g., education, healthcare). "Hand it over."

Part III · Rawls & Nozick

Rawls — Justice as Fairness 1971
1st Principle: equal basic rights for all. 2nd Principle (Difference Principle): inequalities are just only if they benefit the least-advantaged. Thought experiment: Original Position behind the Veil of Ignorance — rational designers who don't know their future position would protect the worst-off. → Supports right to healthcare.
Nozick — Justice as Entitlement 1974
Two libertarian rights: self-ownership & power to transfer property. Three just acquisitions: just transfer (voluntary exchange), just initial acquisition (Lockean proviso — enough & as good left for others), just rectification. Compulsory redistribution = forced labor. → No right to healthcare.

Part III · Allocation

Rescher's 5 Criteria 1969
(1) Likelihood of success · (2) Life-expectancy after treatment · (3) Family role · (4) Future contributions · (5) Past services rendered. "Medical" criteria (1–2) covertly discriminate by age & ability; "social" (3–5) against the solitary & immeasurable.
Moss & Siegler on ARESLD
Argue alcoholics should not compete equally for liver transplants. 3 arguments: Fairness (never had a healthy liver), Personal Effort (treatment was available), Public Support (taxpayer backing at risk). Objections: disease impairs seeking treatment; selective vs motorcyclists, obesity.
Harris's Fair Innings
Anti-ageist: every "rest of life" has equal indefinite value. Fair innings (≈70): dying before is a tragedy, past it is not. Age IS a factor when one candidate is past threshold (89 vs 2). Age IS NOT a factor when both are below (20 vs 50). 7-minute analogy: 3 vs 4 minutes is equally unjust.

Part IV · Genetics Map

SGT / SGE / GLGT / GLGE
Somatic = this person only · Germline = descendants too. Therapy = cure · Enhancement = augment. Standard view: only SGT permissible. Resnik challenges this.
Eugenics — two kinds
State-sponsored (coercive, immoral — Nazi, US, Canada, Sweden). Parental (mate selection, donor choice — routine & moral).

Arguments in Standard Form

Purdy — Reproductive Risk
  1. High risk of harm → moral obligation to prevent it.
  2. Procreating with high risk of serious disease = high risk to the child.
  3. Moral obligation not to procreate when risk is high.
Harris — Anti-Ageist
  1. Two lives do not differ in value due to age.
  2. If no value difference, denying the older is unjust.
  3. Unjust to deny the older patient.
Harris — Fair Innings
  1. Not reasonable to live past one's fair innings at the expense of someone who hasn't reached theirs.
  2. Not unjust to save the younger if they haven't reached theirs.

Resnik's 5 Claimed Differences all false

1. Therapy promotes health; enhancement doesn't. False — plastic surgery, braces are permitted.
2. Therapy fits medicine's goals. False — "health" is contested.
3. Enhancement alters humanness. False — changing form isn't inherently wrong.
4. Enhancement violates unborn rights. False — proxy consent works; no right to unmodified germline; may expand open future.
5. Enhancement is eugenics. Only state eugenics is wrong; parental eugenics is routine.

Cases You Must Know

The Surgeon counterex · Utility
Kill 1 healthy to save 5. Utility requires it; justice forbids it.
The Sheriff counterex · Utility
Frame 1 innocent to appease a lynch mob. Utility requires it; rights forbid it.
Inquiring Murderer counterex · Kant
Kant forbids lying to save a friend from a murderer. Absoluteness produces grotesque result.
The Lifeboat counterex · Kant
Throw the dying man overboard to save 11? Kant: no — using him as a means.
Elizabeth Bouvia 1986
Quadriplegic refused feeding; California court ruled competent adults have a constitutional right to refuse care. Intentions are irrelevant.
Tuskegee Study 1932–1972
399 African American men left untreated for syphilis for 40 years. Triggered the Belmont Report.
BIID Mill test case
Body Integrity Identity Disorder — desire for amputation of a healthy limb. Stress-tests Mill's harm principle. Parallel to gender dysphoria, not a paraphilia.
Mr. Johnson's Toe
Gangrenous diabetic refuses amputation. Means-disagreement → persuade. Values-disagreement → respect.
