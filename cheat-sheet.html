Part I · Moral Theories 5

Utilitarianism Bentham · J.S. Mill
Principle of Utility: an action is morally required if it maximizes good and minimizes bad for all those affected. Classical form uses hedonism (pleasure = good, pain = bad). Weakness: Surgeon & Sheriff — utility can demand injustice.
Kantian Ethics Immanuel Kant
Only the good will has moral worth. The Categorical Imperative has two formulas:
F-I (Universal Law): act only on maxims you can will to become a universal law.
F-II (Humanity): treat persons as ends, never merely as means.
Weakness: Inquiring Murderer, Lifeboat.
Virtue Ethics Aristotle
An act is right because a virtuous person, acting in character, would do it. Virtues are deeply embedded character traits (honesty, courage, justice). Virtue sits at the golden mean between two vices. Phronesis = practical wisdom, the master virtue. Weakness: vague — who counts as the moral exemplar?
Divine Command Theory
An act is right just because God commands it. Two problems: (1) God may not exist; (2) the Euthyphro Dilemma — either God commands what is already right (DCT collapses into another theory) or morality is arbitrary (God could make murder right).
Natural Law Theory Aquinas (1225–1274)
An act is right if consistent with natural purpose. Human purposes: life, procreation, knowledge, society, seeking God. Reason discovers the law. Weakness: Hume's Is–Ought gap — what's natural isn't always good (sexual aggression); what's unnatural isn't always bad (glasses, contraception).

Part II · Belmont 3 principles

Respect for Persons
Acknowledge people's capacity for self-determination; protect those whose autonomy is diminished.
Beneficence
Do no harm; maximize benefits and minimize harms.
Justice
Distribute the benefits and burdens of research equitably, free of bias.

Autonomy & Consent

Autonomy autos + nomos = self-rule
Rational self-determination. Three conditions for an autonomous decision: competence, adequate information (actually understood), freedom from coercion. If any fails, the decision is not autonomous — even for competent adults.
Informed Consent
Autonomous authorization of a medical intervention. Same three conditions as autonomy. Consent is a process, not a form — ongoing throughout treatment.
Paternalism
Overriding autonomy "for a person's own good." Justified for children & severely impaired. Not justified for competent adults — even if their refusal will lead to death.
Mill's Harm Principle On Liberty, 1859
The only acceptable reason for interfering with individual liberty is to prevent harm to others. Their own good or happiness is not sufficient. Rules out paternalism for competent adults.

Higgs & Wicclair

Higgs on Truth-Telling
Lying to patients is almost never permissible. Rejects 3 defenses: complexity, distress, protection from harm. Answer is better telling, not lying. "Lying must be a last resort."
Wicclair's 3 Positions conscientious objection
Absolutism: providers may always refuse. Incompatibilism: refusal is incompatible with professional duty. Reasonable accommodation (middle ground): refuse only if you refer, and the referral doesn't burden the patient.

Cases You Must Know Parts I & II

The Surgeon counterex · Utility
Kill 1 healthy patient to harvest organs, save 5 dying patients. Utility requires it; justice forbids it.
The Sheriff counterex · Utility
Frame 1 innocent Black man to appease a lynch mob and save many. Utility requires it; rights forbid it.
Inquiring Murderer counterex · Kant
A murderer asks where your friend is hiding. Kant forbids lying even to save a life. Absolute duty produces a grotesque result.
The Lifeboat counterex · Kant F-II
11 on a lifeboat built for 10; one is dying from a head wound. Throwing him overboard would save the others — Kant says no, it treats him as a means.
Elizabeth Bouvia 1986
Quadriplegic refused feeding. California Court of Appeal ruled competent adults have a constitutional right to refuse care. Intentions are irrelevant.
Tuskegee Study 1932 — 1972
399 African American men left untreated for syphilis for 40 years. Triggered the Belmont Report.
Mr. Johnson's Toe
Diabetic refuses amputation of a gangrenous toe. If refusal is about means → persuade. If refusal is about values (bodily integrity) → respect.
BIID Mill test case
Body Integrity Identity Disorder — desire for amputation of a healthy limb. Stress-tests Mill's harm principle. Parallel to gender dysphoria (not a sexual paraphilia).

Part III · Rights

Negative Right
Imposes a duty not to interfere (e.g., private property). "Hands off."
Positive Right
Imposes a duty to provide a good or service (e.g., education, healthcare). "Hand it over."

Rawls & Nozick distributive justice

Rawls — Justice as Fairness 1971
1st Principle: equal basic rights & liberties for all.
2nd Principle (Difference Principle): inequalities are just only if they benefit the least-advantaged.
Original Position behind the Veil of Ignorance: rational designers who don't know their future position would protect the worst-off. → Supports a right to healthcare.
Nozick — Justice as Entitlement 1974
Two libertarian rights: self-ownership & power to transfer.
Three just acquisitions: transfer (voluntary exchange), initial acquisition (with Lockean proviso — "enough & as good" left for others), rectification.
Compulsory redistribution = forced labor. → No right to healthcare.

Allocation

Rescher's 5 Criteria 1969
(1) Likelihood of success · (2) Life-expectancy after treatment · (3) Family role · (4) Future contributions · (5) Past services.
"Medical" criteria (1–2) covertly discriminate by age & ability. "Social" (3–5) discriminate against solitary people & can't be objectively measured.
Moss & Siegler · ARESLD
Argue alcoholics should not compete equally for liver transplants. 3 arguments: Fairness (others never had a healthy liver), Personal Effort (treatment was available), Public Support. Objections: disease impairs treatment-seeking; inconsistent vs. motorcyclists & obesity.

Harris · Fair Innings

Anti-Ageist Argument
Every person's "rest of life" has equal indefinite value — we don't know how long any of us have left. Denying the older person is unjust on age alone.
Fair Innings
≈70 years. Dying before = tragedy; dying past it = misfortune but not unjust. Age IS a factor when one candidate has passed the threshold (89 vs. 2). Age is NOT a factor when both are below (20 vs. 50). The 7-minute analogy: whether you get 3 or 4 minutes is equally unjust.

Part IV · Genetics

SGT · SGE · GLGT · GLGE
Somatic = this person only · Germline = descendants too.
Therapy = cure · Enhancement = augment.
Standard view: only SGT is permissible. Resnik challenges this distinction.
Eugenics · Two Kinds
State-sponsored (coercive sterilization in Nazi Germany, U.S., Canada, Sweden) — immoral. Parental (mate selection, donor choice) — routine & moral. Wrongness lies in coercion, not the genetic aim.
Purdy on Reproductive Risk 1996
Case: Huntington's disease. 50% chance of transmission; disease appears 30–50 and ends in death after ~15 years. Purdy: it is impermissible to procreate when risk of serious disease transmission is high. The biological route isn't privileged — adoption & donors are alternatives.
Resnik on Therapy vs. Enhancement
The line is too blurry to ground moral judgments. All 5 alleged differences fail (see Column 3). Most importantly: parental eugenics is routine, so the eugenics worry dissolves.

Arguments in Standard Form

Purdy — Reproductive Risk
  1. High risk of harm → moral obligation to prevent.
  2. Procreating with high risk of serious disease = high risk to the child.
  3. Moral obligation not to procreate when risk is high.
Harris — Anti-Ageist
  1. Two lives do not differ in value due to age.
  2. If no value difference, denying the older is unjust.
  3. Unjust to deny the older patient.
Harris — Fair Innings
  1. Not reasonable to live past one's fair innings at the expense of someone who hasn't reached theirs.
  2. Not unjust to save the younger, if they haven't reached theirs.

Resnik's 5 Alleged Differences all fail

1. "Therapy promotes health; enhancement doesn't."
→ Plastic surgery, braces are permitted and don't promote health.

2. "Therapy fits medicine's goals."
→ "Health" itself is contested; different definitions classify differently.

3. "Enhancement alters humanness."
→ Changing human form isn't inherently wrong.

4. "Enhancement violates unborn rights."
→ Proxy consent works. No meaningful right to unmodified germline. May expand open future.

5. "Enhancement is eugenics."
→ Only coercive state eugenics is wrong; parental is routine.
